# 5. Conclusion

This chapter concludes the thesis by summarizing the key findings in relation to the research question, discussing the theoretical and practical contributions of the work, acknowledging its limitations, and proposing directions for future research. The chapter closes with a reflection on the broader significance of teacher-driven prompt evolution for enterprise AI adoption.

## 7.1 Revisiting the Research Problem

Enterprise customer service represents one of the most demanding deployment environments for LLM-based agents. Organizations require reliability at four-nines (99.99%) or above, yet even frontier models such as Claude Opus 4.5 peak at roughly 98% on structured tool-calling benchmarks like τ²-bench (Barres et al., 2025; Rabanser et al., 2025). The gap between what benchmarks measure and what production demands is not merely numerical—it is qualitative. A 2% failure rate on a benchmark translates into thousands of mishandled customer interactions per day at enterprise scale, each one a potential churn event, compliance violation, or reputational risk. The core obstacle is that deployed agents are static: once their prompt and tool configuration are set, they do not learn from the failures that inevitably occur in operation. Fine-tuning and reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) can improve model behavior at training time (Ouyang et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022), but they are expensive, slow to iterate, and inaccessible to organizations that consume models through APIs without the ability to modify weights. Meanwhile, the humans who already work alongside AI agents in customer service workflows—correcting mistakes, handling escalations, resolving edge cases—generate a rich stream of behavioral data that is almost entirely wasted as a learning signal.

This thesis asked a single research question: **How can AI agent performance on structured benchmarks be improved through automated, teacher-model-driven prompt and tool evolution?** The question was motivated by a gap at the intersection of three literatures—agent benchmarking, automated prompt optimization, and human-in-the-loop supervision—where no prior work had combined teacher-driven diagnosis with prompt-level patching and validated the result on a multi-turn tool-calling benchmark.

## 7.2 Summary of Key Findings

The thesis developed and evaluated a Diagnose-Patch-Validate framework in which a stronger teacher model (Kimi K2.5) analyzes a weaker student model's (Qwen3 30B-A3B) failed conversation traces on τ²-bench, diagnoses the root cause of each failure, proposes structured patches to the student's prompt, tool schemas, or tool preprocessors, and validates those patches through re-simulation before merging. The student model's weights are never modified; only its operating instructions change. Two experiments were conducted on the airline domain of τ²-bench, with a third in progress.

**Finding 1: Teacher-driven prompt evolution produces measurable improvement.** In Experiment 1 (5 tasks), the framework raised the student's trial-level pass rate from 53% to 73%, an absolute gain of 20 percentage points. In Experiment 2 (10 tasks), trial-level pass rate rose from 27% to 50%, a gain of 23 percentage points. These improvements were achieved without any weight updates, gradient computation, or preference data collection—only through structured edits to the student's system prompt and tool interface.

**Finding 2: Instruction-tier patches dominate.** Across both experiments, 71% of successful fixes were prompt-level instruction patches—additions or clarifications of behavioral rules in the system prompt—while the remaining 29% were guardrail-tier patches involving tool schema constraints or input preprocessors. This pattern held precisely constant across the two experiments despite the doubling of task pool size. The dominance of instruction patches aligns with the Superficial Alignment Hypothesis (Zhou et al., 2023): the student model possesses the underlying capability to perform the task correctly but lacks explicit knowledge that it *should* follow a particular policy or procedure. Stating the requirement in plain text is sufficient to unlock the capability in the majority of fixable cases.

**Finding 3: Absolute gain is stable but fix rate declines with scale.** The framework produced approximately the same absolute improvement (~20–23 percentage points) in both experiments, but the fix rate—the fraction of failing tasks that were successfully resolved—dropped from 100% (4 out of 4) in Experiment 1 to 56% (5 out of 9) in Experiment 2. The number of successfully fixed tasks (7 in both experiments, counting across sweeps) suggests that the framework has a roughly constant capacity: it can reliably fix a certain number of tasks per run, but as the pool grows, a larger proportion of failures falls outside the reachable set. The implication is that prompt-level patching has a natural ceiling, and scaling to larger task sets will require complementary methods.

**Finding 4: A hard core of resistant tasks emerges.** Experiment 2 revealed four tasks (7, 9, 11, 12) that resisted all fix attempts across both sweeps, consuming over 150 messages, 61 tool calls, and 36 minutes of teacher compute in Sweep 1 alone without yielding a single successful patch. These tasks appear to require capabilities—multi-step reasoning under uncertainty, implicit policy interpretation, or complex state tracking—that cannot be injected through prompt text alone. Their existence defines the boundary of prompt-level intervention and points toward the need for architectural or training-time solutions for the hardest failure modes.

**Finding 5: The framework saturates rapidly.** In both experiments, diminishing returns set in after the first two sweeps, with Sweep 3 producing zero new fixes and occasional mild regressions. This rapid saturation has practical implications: the framework's value is concentrated in the first one or two passes, making it efficient for deployment but limited in cumulative impact.

**Finding 6: Patch interference is a real risk.** Accumulated patches can degrade performance on previously passing tasks—an effect analogous to catastrophic forgetting in continual learning (Luo et al., 2023), but operating in the prompt space rather than the weight space. In Experiment 1, Sweep 2 saw three tasks regress after patches from Sweep 1 were applied, requiring re-fixing. This suggests that production deployments would need a patch management discipline—versioning, regression testing, and potentially patch retirement—to maintain reliability over time.

**Finding 7: Student model strength reshapes the framework's utility.** Preliminary results with Qwen3.5 Flash as an alternative student showed a perfect 5/5 pass rate on the 5-task subset, eliminating the need for evolution entirely at that scale. This finding indicates that the framework's value is conditional on the baseline gap: for weaker models, it provides substantial uplift by compensating for missing knowledge; for stronger models, its role shifts from fixing fundamentals to polishing edge-case reliability—a narrower but still practically relevant use case.

## 7.3 Contributions to Knowledge

This thesis makes several contributions that, individually, have precedents in the literature but, in combination, are novel.

First, the work demonstrates **teacher-to-student knowledge transfer at the prompt level** rather than the weight level. Classical knowledge distillation (Hinton et al., 2015) and its LLM variants (Sanh et al., 2019; Jiao et al., 2020; Taori et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023) operate by training a smaller model to mimic a larger one's outputs. The framework presented here achieves an analogous transfer—a stronger model's diagnostic knowledge improving a weaker model's behavior—without modifying the student's parameters. This makes the approach compatible with API-only model access, a constraint that applies to the vast majority of enterprise LLM deployments.

Second, the thesis provides the **first empirical evaluation of automated prompt optimization on a multi-turn tool-calling benchmark**. Prior automated prompt evolution work, including GEPA (Agrawal et al., 2025), DSPy (Khattab et al., 2023), TextGrad (Yuksekgonul et al., 2024), and PromptBreeder (Fernando et al., 2023), was evaluated on reasoning tasks, classification problems, or single-turn QA. None tested their methods on benchmarks that combine multi-turn dialogue, simulated users, tool calling, and domain-specific policy compliance. By validating on τ²-bench, this thesis extends the applicability boundary of prompt evolution to agentic settings.

Third, the framework introduces **three distinct patch surfaces**—system prompt rules, tool schema annotations, and sandboxed tool preprocessors—and provides empirical evidence on the relative effectiveness of each. The 71/29 instruction-to-guardrail split across experiments quantifies the contribution of each surface and offers actionable guidance for practitioners: start with prompt-level policy statements, escalate to tool-level constraints only when necessary.

Fourth, the experiments document **scaling behavior** that was previously uncharacterized in the prompt optimization literature: the constancy of absolute gain, the decline of fix rate, the emergence of a hard core of resistant tasks, the rapid saturation across sweeps, and the risk of patch interference. These empirical patterns constitute a contribution in their own right, providing a basis for predicting the framework's performance in larger-scale deployments and for designing mitigation strategies.

## 7.4 Practical Implications

For enterprise teams deploying LLM agents in customer service, the framework offers several practical advantages. It operates entirely in the input space—patches are human-readable text edits to prompts and JSON schema annotations—which means they can be reviewed, versioned, approved through existing change-management workflows, and rolled back if problems arise. No GPU infrastructure is needed for the student model beyond inference, and the teacher model can be accessed through an API for the diagnostic phase. The diagnose-patch-validate loop can be integrated into existing quality assurance pipelines: when an agent fails a customer interaction (detected through automated evaluation or human review), the failure trace is routed to the teacher for diagnosis, a candidate patch is generated, validated against held-out tasks, and deployed if it passes regression checks.

The finding that instruction-tier patches dominate has a specific implication for how enterprises should think about agent customization. Rather than investing in fine-tuning to encode domain-specific policies, organizations may achieve comparable gains by investing in thorough policy documentation and structured prompt engineering—activities that are already part of standard customer service operations. The framework automates the translation from "here is what went wrong" to "here is the prompt edit that prevents it," reducing the manual prompt-fiddling bottleneck identified in the literature review.

At the same time, the limitations are practically important. The framework's fix rate declines as the task pool grows, and a hard core of failures resists all prompt-level intervention. This means the framework is best understood as one layer in a defense-in-depth strategy: it handles the "long tail" of policy-encodable failures efficiently, but enterprise reliability at four-nines or above will require additional layers—stronger base models, fine-tuning for the hardest cases, architectural improvements to tool-use pipelines, and human escalation for irreducible edge cases.

## 7.5 Theoretical Implications

The results contribute to several ongoing theoretical discussions. The consistent dominance of instruction-tier patches provides further empirical support for the Superficial Alignment Hypothesis (Zhou et al., 2023), extending it from single-turn instruction-following to multi-turn tool-calling settings. If a small number of plain-text additions to the system prompt can recover 20+ percentage points of benchmark performance, the implication is that the student model's pre-training already encodes most of the necessary competence; what is missing is a thin layer of task-specific behavioral specification. This resonates with the broader finding from Sclar et al. (2023) that prompt formatting alone can swing performance by up to 76 percentage points—but with an important distinction: the patches generated by the teacher model are semantically meaningful, targeted interventions rather than arbitrary formatting variations.

The emergence of patch interference connects the work to the continual learning literature. Just as neural networks suffer from catastrophic forgetting when trained on a sequence of tasks (Luo et al., 2023), prompt-based agents can suffer from "prompt-space forgetting" when a sequence of patches optimized for individual failure cases degrades collective behavior. This analogy suggests that techniques developed for continual learning—such as elastic weight consolidation or experience replay—may have prompt-level counterparts worth exploring: for example, maintaining a validation suite of previously passing tasks as a regression buffer, or regularizing new patches against the existing prompt to minimize interference.

The framework also extends the concept of knowledge distillation into a new modality. Where Hinton et al. (2015) proposed distilling a teacher's knowledge into a student's weights, this thesis demonstrates distillation into a student's *operating instructions*—a form of transfer that preserves the student's original capabilities while augmenting its behavior in targeted areas. This "prompt-level distillation" paradigm may prove especially relevant as the industry moves toward a model marketplace where practitioners combine base models from one provider with prompt configurations optimized by another.

## 7.6 Limitations

Several limitations constrain the generalizability of the findings and should be acknowledged transparently.

**Domain coverage.** Both experiments were conducted exclusively on the airline domain of τ²-bench. While this domain is representative of structured customer service tasks (booking changes, policy lookups, multi-step modifications), the retail and telecom domains of the benchmark remain untested. Different domains may present different failure distributions and different responsiveness to prompt-level patching. The planned Experiment 3 (20 airline tasks) and subsequent cross-domain experiments will partially address this gap, but the current results should not be generalized across domains without further evidence.

**Teacher-student pair.** The framework was evaluated with a single teacher (Kimi K2.5) and a single student (Qwen3 30B-A3B). Different teacher models may produce qualitatively different diagnoses, and different students may respond differently to the same patches. The preliminary Qwen3.5 Flash results already demonstrate that student strength dramatically affects the framework's utility. A systematic exploration of the teacher-student design space is needed to characterize the framework's robustness.

**Statistical power.** Each task was evaluated with only 3 trials per condition, yielding majority-vote pass/fail decisions. This provides limited statistical resolution for detecting small effects and makes the results sensitive to individual run variance. A more rigorous evaluation would use 10–20 trials per task, enabling confidence intervals and significance testing.

**User simulator confound.** The τ²-bench user simulator that generates the conversational partner during evaluation is itself an LLM. If the simulator shares model-family biases with the student (both are from the open-source ecosystem), the benchmark results may not fully reflect performance with real human users. This is a limitation of the benchmark itself rather than the framework, but it affects the ecological validity of the findings.

**No comparison baselines.** The experiments compare the evolved agent against the unmodified baseline and the frontier ceiling, but do not compare against alternative improvement methods such as RLHF, DPO (Rafailov et al., 2023), or LoRA fine-tuning (Hu et al., 2022). Without such comparisons, the relative efficiency of prompt evolution versus training-time methods remains an open question.

**Cumulative patching without retirement.** The current implementation accumulates all accepted patches without any mechanism for retiring, consolidating, or pruning patches that become redundant or counterproductive over time. The observed patch interference suggests that an unbounded patch accumulation strategy will eventually degrade performance, but the experiments were too short (3 sweeps) to characterize the long-term dynamics.

**Distance from enterprise reliability.** The best trial-level pass rate achieved was 73% on 5 tasks. While this represents meaningful improvement from a 53% baseline, it remains far from the 99.99% reliability that motivates the work. The framework demonstrably moves the needle, but it is not, by itself, a solution to the enterprise reliability problem.

## 7.7 Directions for Future Research

The limitations above suggest several concrete directions for future investigation.

**Cross-domain and cross-benchmark evaluation.** Extending the framework to the retail and telecom domains of τ²-bench, as well as to related benchmarks such as CRMArena (Huang et al., 2025) and τ-bench (Yao et al., 2024), would establish whether the observed patterns—instruction-patch dominance, fix-rate decline, rapid saturation—generalize or are specific to the airline domain.

**Teacher-student ablations.** Systematically varying the teacher model (e.g., GPT-4 class models, Claude, open-source alternatives) and the student model (across model families, sizes, and architectural choices) would map the design space and identify the minimum teacher-student capability gap required for effective prompt-level transfer.

**Hybrid prompt-and-weight evolution.** The hard core of resistant tasks points toward a natural extension: combining prompt evolution with lightweight fine-tuning. A two-stage pipeline could apply prompt patches for the easy wins, then use the remaining failures as training signal for LoRA or adapter-based fine-tuning (Hu et al., 2022). This would test whether the two approaches are complementary—addressing different failure modes—or whether prompt evolution is simply a weaker form of the same intervention.

**Patch management and consolidation.** The patch interference finding motivates research into prompt-space equivalents of continual learning techniques. Potential approaches include periodic patch consolidation (merging multiple patches into a single coherent rewrite), regression-aware patch selection (accepting patches only if they do not degrade a held-out validation set), and patch retirement policies that remove patches whose effects have been superseded.

**Integration with GEPA and related methods.** The closest methodological precedent to this work is GEPA (Agrawal et al., 2025), which performs reflective prompt evolution using the model's own traces rather than an external teacher. A direct comparison—and potential hybrid—between teacher-driven diagnosis and self-reflective optimization would clarify the relative value of external supervision versus self-improvement in the agentic prompt optimization setting.

**Real-world deployment study.** The ultimate test of the framework is deployment in a production customer service environment, where the teacher model processes real failure traces from real customer interactions and proposes patches that are validated against real-world success criteria. Such a study would address the ecological validity limitations of benchmark-only evaluation and provide evidence on the framework's practical impact on customer satisfaction, agent efficiency, and operational cost.

## 7.8 Closing Reflection

This thesis began with a simple observation: LLM agents fail in customer service, and humans already know how to fix those failures—but the knowledge flows in only one direction, from human to customer, rather than from human to agent. The Diagnose-Patch-Validate framework closes part of that loop. It demonstrates that a teacher model can extract actionable learning from failure traces and translate it into structured edits that measurably improve a student agent's performance—without touching model weights, without collecting preference data, and without retraining.

The improvements are real but bounded. Prompt-level patching captures the low-hanging fruit: missing policy statements, ambiguous tool interfaces, and procedural gaps that the student model can execute correctly once told to do so. It does not—and cannot—compensate for fundamental capability limitations in reasoning, state tracking, or implicit knowledge that the student model simply does not possess. The hard core of resistant tasks is a reminder that the path from 73% to 99.99% will require advances at every layer of the stack: stronger base models, better training methods, smarter architectures, and robust human-AI collaboration.

What the framework does offer is a principled, lightweight, and immediately deployable mechanism for continuous improvement in the input space—one that is compatible with the constraints under which most enterprises actually operate. In a landscape where model weights are increasingly locked behind APIs and competitive moats, the ability to systematically improve agent behavior through diagnostic, human-readable prompt engineering may prove to be not just a stopgap, but a durable and complementary paradigm for enterprise AI reliability.
