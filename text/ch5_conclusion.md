# 5. Conclusion

This chapter summarizes the findings, discusses what they mean for theory and practice, acknowledges limitations, and suggests directions for future work.

## 5.1 Revisiting the research problem

Enterprise customer service is a hard environment for LLM-based agents. Organizations want reliability at four-nines (99.99%) or above, but even frontier models like Claude Opus 4.5 top out around 98% on structured tool-calling benchmarks like τ²-bench [@barres2025; @rabanser2025]. That 2% gap matters more than it sounds. At enterprise scale, it translates into thousands of mishandled customer interactions per day, any of which could mean churn, compliance violations, or reputational damage. The problem is that deployed agents are static: once their prompt and tool configuration are set, they do not learn from the failures that follow. Fine-tuning and RLHF can improve model behavior at training time [@ouyang2022; @bai2022], but they are expensive, slow, and inaccessible to organizations consuming models through APIs without the ability to modify weights. Meanwhile, the humans working alongside these agents in customer service workflows already correct mistakes, handle escalations, and resolve edge cases every day. That behavioral data is almost entirely unused as a learning signal.

This thesis asked a single research question: **How can AI agent performance on structured benchmarks be improved through automated, teacher-model-driven prompt and tool evolution?** The question sits at the intersection of three literatures: agent benchmarking, automated prompt optimization, and human-in-the-loop supervision. No prior work had combined teacher-driven diagnosis with prompt-level patching and validated the result on a multi-turn tool-calling benchmark.

## 5.2 Summary of findings

The thesis developed and evaluated a Diagnose-Patch-Validate framework in which a stronger teacher model (Kimi K2.5) analyzes a weaker student model's (Qwen3 30B-A3B) failed conversation traces on τ²-bench, diagnoses root causes, proposes structured patches to the student's prompt, tool schemas, or tool preprocessors, and validates those patches through re-simulation before merging. The student model's weights are never modified; only its operating instructions change. Two experiments were conducted on the airline domain of τ²-bench, with a third in progress.

**Finding 1: Prompt evolution produces measurable improvement.** In Experiment 1 (5 tasks), the framework raised the student's trial-level pass rate from 53% to 73%, an absolute gain of 20 percentage points. In Experiment 2 (10 tasks), trial-level pass rate rose from 27% to 50%, a gain of 23 percentage points. These improvements came without weight updates, gradient computation, or preference data, only through structured edits to the student's system prompt and tool interface.

**Finding 2: Instruction-tier patches dominate.** Across both experiments, 71% of successful fixes were prompt-level instruction patches (additions or clarifications of behavioral rules in the system prompt), while the remaining 29% were guardrail-tier patches involving tool schema constraints or input preprocessors. This 71/29 split held precisely constant across both experiments despite doubling the task pool size. The dominance of instruction patches fits the Superficial Alignment Hypothesis [@zhou2023lima]: the student model already has the capability to perform correctly but lacks explicit knowledge that it *should* follow a particular policy. Stating the requirement in plain text is enough to unlock the capability in most fixable cases.

**Finding 3: Absolute gain is stable but fix rate declines with scale.** The framework produced roughly the same absolute improvement (~20-23 percentage points) in both experiments, but the fix rate dropped from 100% (4 out of 4) in Experiment 1 to 56% (5 out of 9) in Experiment 2. The number of successfully fixed tasks (7 in both experiments, counting across sweeps) suggests a roughly constant capacity: the framework can reliably fix a certain number of tasks per run, but as the pool grows, a larger share of failures falls outside reach. Prompt-level patching has a natural ceiling, and scaling to larger task sets will require complementary methods.

**Finding 4: A hard core of resistant tasks emerges.** Experiment 2 revealed four tasks (7, 9, 11, 12) that resisted all fix attempts across both sweeps, consuming over 150 messages, 61 tool calls, and 36 minutes of teacher compute in Sweep 1 alone without yielding a single successful patch. These tasks appear to require capabilities like multi-step reasoning under uncertainty, implicit policy interpretation, or complex state tracking that cannot be injected through prompt text. Their existence defines the boundary of what prompt-level intervention can do.

**Finding 5: The framework saturates rapidly.** In both experiments, diminishing returns set in after the first two sweeps, with Sweep 3 producing zero new fixes and occasional mild regressions. The practical implication: the framework's value is concentrated in the first one or two passes, making it efficient for deployment but limited in cumulative impact.

**Finding 6: Patch interference is real.** Accumulated patches can degrade performance on previously passing tasks, an effect analogous to catastrophic forgetting in continual learning [@luo2023], but operating in prompt space rather than weight space. In Experiment 1, Sweep 2 saw three tasks regress after patches from Sweep 1 were applied, requiring re-fixing. Production deployments would need patch management discipline: versioning, regression testing, and potentially patch retirement to maintain reliability over time.

**Finding 7: Student model strength reshapes the framework's utility.** Preliminary results with Qwen3.5 Flash as an alternative student showed a perfect 5/5 pass rate on the 5-task subset, eliminating the need for evolution entirely at that scale. The framework's value depends on the baseline gap: for weaker models, it provides substantial uplift by compensating for missing knowledge; for stronger models, its role narrows to polishing edge-case reliability, which is still practically relevant but a different proposition.

## 5.3 Contributions to knowledge

This thesis makes several contributions that individually have precedents but, in combination, are novel.

First, it demonstrates **teacher-to-student knowledge transfer at the prompt level** rather than the weight level. Classical knowledge distillation [@hinton2015] and its LLM variants [@sanh2019; @jiao2020; @taori2023; @xu2023wizard] train a smaller model to mimic a larger one's outputs. The framework here achieves a similar transfer, with a stronger model's diagnostic knowledge improving a weaker model's behavior, without modifying the student's parameters. This makes the approach compatible with API-only model access, which is how most enterprises actually use LLMs.

Second, the thesis provides the **first empirical evaluation of automated prompt optimization on a multi-turn tool-calling benchmark**. Prior work on automated prompt evolution, including GEPA [@agrawal2025], DSPy [@khattab2023], TextGrad [@yuksekgonul2024], and PromptBreeder [@fernando2023], was evaluated on reasoning tasks, classification problems, or single-turn QA. None tested their methods on benchmarks combining multi-turn dialogue, simulated users, tool calling, and domain-specific policy compliance. By validating on τ²-bench, this thesis extends prompt evolution into agentic settings.

Third, the framework introduces **three distinct patch surfaces**: system prompt rules, tool schema annotations, and sandboxed tool preprocessors. The empirical 71/29 instruction-to-guardrail split across experiments quantifies the relative effectiveness of each surface and gives practitioners a clear heuristic: start with prompt-level policy statements, escalate to tool-level constraints only when necessary.

Fourth, the experiments document **scaling behavior** previously uncharacterized in the prompt optimization literature: constant absolute gain, declining fix rate, the hard core of resistant tasks, rapid saturation across sweeps, and patch interference. These patterns provide a basis for predicting the framework's performance at larger scale and designing mitigation strategies.

## 5.4 Practical implications

For enterprise teams deploying LLM agents in customer service, the framework has several practical advantages. It operates entirely in the input space: patches are human-readable text edits to prompts and JSON schema annotations. They can be reviewed, versioned, approved through existing change-management workflows, and rolled back. No GPU infrastructure is needed for the student model beyond inference, and the teacher model can be accessed through an API for the diagnostic phase. The diagnose-patch-validate loop fits into existing quality assurance pipelines: when an agent fails a customer interaction (detected through automated evaluation or human review), the failure trace goes to the teacher for diagnosis, a candidate patch is generated, validated against held-out tasks, and deployed if it passes regression checks.

The dominance of instruction-tier patches has a specific implication. Rather than investing in fine-tuning to encode domain-specific policies, organizations may achieve comparable gains by investing in thorough policy documentation and structured prompt engineering, activities already part of standard customer service operations. The framework automates the translation from "here is what went wrong" to "here is the prompt edit that prevents it," reducing the manual prompt-fiddling bottleneck identified in the literature review.

The limitations matter too. The fix rate declines as the task pool grows, and a hard core of failures resists all prompt-level intervention. The framework is best understood as one layer in a broader strategy: it handles the long tail of policy-encodable failures efficiently, but four-nines reliability will require additional layers, including stronger base models, fine-tuning for the hardest cases, architectural improvements to tool-use pipelines, and human escalation for irreducible edge cases.

## 5.5 Theoretical implications

The results speak to several ongoing theoretical discussions. The consistent dominance of instruction-tier patches provides further empirical support for the Superficial Alignment Hypothesis [@zhou2023lima], extending it from single-turn instruction-following to multi-turn tool-calling settings. If a small number of plain-text additions to the system prompt can recover 20+ percentage points of benchmark performance, the student model's pre-training already encodes most of the necessary competence; what is missing is a thin layer of task-specific behavioral specification. This connects to the finding from @sclar2023 that prompt formatting alone can swing performance by up to 76 percentage points, but with a difference: the patches here are semantically meaningful, targeted interventions rather than arbitrary formatting variations.

Patch interference connects the work to the continual learning literature. Just as neural networks suffer from catastrophic forgetting when trained on a sequence of tasks [@luo2023], prompt-based agents can suffer from "prompt-space forgetting" when patches optimized for individual failure cases degrade collective behavior. This analogy suggests that techniques from continual learning, such as elastic weight consolidation or experience replay, may have prompt-level counterparts worth exploring: maintaining a validation suite of previously passing tasks as a regression buffer, or regularizing new patches against the existing prompt to minimize interference.

The framework also extends knowledge distillation into a new modality. Where @hinton2015 proposed distilling a teacher's knowledge into a student's weights, this thesis demonstrates distillation into a student's *operating instructions*, a form of transfer that preserves the student's original capabilities while augmenting its behavior in targeted areas. This "prompt-level distillation" could matter as the industry moves toward a model marketplace where practitioners combine base models from one provider with prompt configurations optimized by another.

## 5.6 Limitations

Several limitations constrain the generalizability of these findings.

**Domain coverage.** Both experiments used only the airline domain of τ²-bench. While this domain is representative of structured customer service tasks (booking changes, policy lookups, multi-step modifications), the retail and telecom domains remain untested. Different domains may present different failure distributions and respond differently to prompt-level patching. The planned Experiment 3 (20 airline tasks) and subsequent cross-domain experiments will partially address this, but the current results should not be generalized across domains without further evidence.

**Teacher-student pair.** The framework was evaluated with a single teacher (Kimi K2.5) and a single student (Qwen3 30B-A3B). Different teacher models may produce qualitatively different diagnoses, and different students may respond differently to the same patches. The preliminary Qwen3.5 Flash results already show that student strength dramatically affects the framework's utility. Systematic exploration of the teacher-student design space is needed.

**Statistical power.** Each task was evaluated with only 3 trials per condition, yielding majority-vote pass/fail decisions. This gives limited resolution for detecting small effects and makes results sensitive to individual run variance. A more rigorous evaluation would use 10-20 trials per task, enabling confidence intervals and significance testing.

**User simulator confound.** The τ²-bench user simulator is itself an LLM. If the simulator shares model-family biases with the student (both are from the open-source ecosystem), benchmark results may not fully reflect performance with real human users. This is a limitation of the benchmark, not the framework, but it affects ecological validity.

**No comparison baselines.** The experiments compare the evolved agent against the unmodified baseline and the frontier ceiling, but not against alternative improvement methods such as RLHF, DPO [@rafailov2023], or LoRA fine-tuning [@hu2022]. Without such comparisons, the relative efficiency of prompt evolution versus training-time methods remains open.

**Cumulative patching without retirement.** The current implementation accumulates all accepted patches without any mechanism for retiring, consolidating, or pruning patches that become redundant or counterproductive. The observed patch interference suggests that unbounded accumulation will eventually degrade performance, but the experiments were too short (3 sweeps) to characterize the long-term dynamics.

**Distance from enterprise reliability.** The best trial-level pass rate achieved was 73% on 5 tasks. While this is a meaningful improvement from a 53% baseline, it is far from the 99.99% that motivates the work. The framework helps, but it is not, by itself, a solution to the enterprise reliability problem.

## 5.7 Directions for future research

The limitations above suggest several concrete directions.

**Cross-domain and cross-benchmark evaluation.** Extending the framework to the retail and telecom domains of τ²-bench, and to related benchmarks such as CRMArena [@huang2025crmarena] and τ-bench [@yao2024], would establish whether the observed patterns (instruction-patch dominance, fix-rate decline, rapid saturation) generalize or are specific to the airline domain.

**Teacher-student ablations.** Systematically varying the teacher model (GPT-4 class models, Claude, open-source alternatives) and the student model (across families, sizes, and architectures) would map the design space and identify the minimum teacher-student capability gap required for effective prompt-level transfer.

**Hybrid prompt-and-weight evolution.** The resistant tasks point toward a natural extension: combining prompt evolution with lightweight fine-tuning. A two-stage pipeline could apply prompt patches for the easy wins, then use the remaining failures as training signal for LoRA or adapter-based fine-tuning [@hu2022]. This would test whether the two approaches are complementary, addressing different failure modes, or whether prompt evolution is simply a weaker form of the same intervention.

**Patch management and consolidation.** The patch interference finding motivates research into prompt-space equivalents of continual learning techniques. Possibilities include periodic patch consolidation (merging multiple patches into a single coherent rewrite), regression-aware patch selection (accepting patches only if they do not degrade a held-out validation set), and patch retirement policies that remove patches whose effects have been superseded.

**Integration with GEPA and related methods.** The closest methodological precedent is GEPA [@agrawal2025], which performs reflective prompt evolution using the model's own traces rather than an external teacher. A direct comparison, and potential hybrid, between teacher-driven diagnosis and self-reflective optimization would clarify the relative value of external supervision versus self-improvement in agentic prompt optimization.

**Real-world deployment study.** The real test of the framework is deployment in a production customer service environment, where the teacher model processes actual failure traces from actual customer interactions and proposes patches validated against real-world success criteria. Such a study would address the ecological validity limitations of benchmark-only evaluation and provide evidence on impact on customer satisfaction, agent efficiency, and operational cost.

## 5.8 Closing reflection

This thesis started from a simple observation: LLM agents fail in customer service, and humans already know how to fix those failures, but the knowledge flows in only one direction, from human to customer, not from human to agent. The Diagnose-Patch-Validate framework closes part of that loop. A teacher model can extract actionable learning from failure traces and translate it into structured edits that measurably improve a student agent's performance, all without touching model weights or retraining.

The improvements are real but bounded. Prompt-level patching captures the accessible failures: missing policy statements, ambiguous tool interfaces, and procedural gaps that the student model can handle correctly once told to do so. It cannot compensate for gaps in reasoning, state tracking, or implicit knowledge that the student model does not possess. The resistant tasks make clear that getting from 73% to 99.99% will take work at every layer: stronger base models, better training, smarter architectures, and real human-AI collaboration.

What the framework does offer is a lightweight mechanism for continuous improvement in the input space that works within the constraints most enterprises actually face: no access to weights, no training infrastructure, limited ML expertise. As model weights get locked further behind APIs, systematic prompt engineering based on failure diagnosis may stick around longer than the "prompt engineering is a fad" crowd expects.
